# Engineering manager guide

## Why you should care

Adoption changes team habits, review load, training needs, and risk. The manager
ensures the harness improves delivery rather than becoming unmeasured ceremony.

## Where you participate

Select pilot work, define team ownership and review expectations, protect
training time, monitor workload and outcomes, resolve process impediments, and
recommend continue, adjust, or stop.

## Inputs and outputs

Inputs: team baseline, approved tools, repository constraints, risk profile,
capacity, metrics, and incident evidence. Outputs: pilot charter, team
responsibility assignment matrix (RACI),
training and support plan, required checks, review capacity, and adoption
recommendation.

## Decisions you own

Own team process, capacity allocation, engineering standards, and escalation
within delegated authority. Portfolio investment and organization risk belong
to senior leadership.

## Common mistakes

- Measuring generated volume instead of delivery outcomes and correction cost.
- Ranking individuals by artificial intelligence (AI) metrics.
- Starting with the riskiest repository or an organization-wide mandate.
- Adding gates without removing low-value ceremony.

## Example workflow

Baseline one team and repository; choose representative low-to-medium-risk
changes; define stop conditions and named support; run the pilot; review lead
time, rework, defects, review burden, and confidence; scale only after evidence
and control gaps are resolved.

## Review checklist

- Approved hosts, data classes, and permissions are explicit.
- Training and expert support are funded.
- Required checks and reviewer independence are clear.
- Pilot metrics include cost and user experience.
- Incidents and exceptions have owners and rollback.
- The team can stop using the harness without losing authoritative artifacts.
